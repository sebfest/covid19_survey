10 The root of the problem is a medical one not an economic one. Infection control measures will have implications for the economy. The trade-off is inherent and not a "damage" to the economy. A lack of control measures will also damage the economy. First, government must deal with the medical part of the problem (testing, ensuring protective gear for medical staff, hospital space, etc.). However, given the uncertainty with how long this will last, its is most important to provide stability for small/medium enterprises (SME) and households (HH). SME will have reduction in revenue and cash flow issues (utility payments, rent, salaries, etc.) They will need to be supported now through various schemes (some loans, some grants). HH will need to be supported primarily those who have lost jobs as a result of the virus and those who were previously unemployed and looking for work. Income support to those HH are necessary. Non-means tested support should not be a policy response. Larger enterprises will also have to be supported through loan guarantees that have less state backing (i.e., easing their cash flow without providing outright grants and with conditions on maintaining employment, if relevant). The virus is a temporary reduction in aggregate demand which also resulted in a reduction in aggregate supply (through a reduction in labor force activity). Once the medical part of the shock is resolved, the policies should be to ensure aggregate demand is supported, which should support the return of the active labor force. The wording of some of the questions can be difficult to answer properly because the scenario depends so much on the medical situation.
